Sandbox Controls

📖 What is Total Internal Reflection? When light travels from a denser medium (higher index n₁) to a rarer one (lower n₂), it bends away from the normal. Past a certain critical angle θc = sin⁻¹(n₂/n₁) the refracted ray would need to bend more than 90° — impossible — so 100% of the light reflects back. That is Total Internal Reflection (TIR). Below θc, light splits into a reflected part (R) and a refracted part (T) given by the Fresnel equations.

Fiber Optic Controls

📖 How an Optical Fiber Works A fiber is a thin core of high-index glass wrapped in lower-index cladding. Light bouncing inside hits the core/cladding boundary; if its angle there exceeds the critical angle θc = sin⁻¹(n_clad/n_core) it totally reflects and stays trapped — this is how a signal travels kilometers with almost no loss. The Numerical Aperture NA = √(n_core² − n_clad²) sets the acceptance cone. Bend the fiber or shrink the index gap and the wall angle drops below θc → light leaks out (orange rays).
The inner glass. Must have the higher index for guiding.

Prism Controls

📖 Prisms: Refraction & Total Internal Reflection A right-angle prism bends a beam by 90° using a single TIR off its hypotenuse — but only if the glass index is high enough that the 45° hit exceeds the critical angle (needs n ≥ √2 ≈ 1.414). A Porro prism uses two TIRs to send light back 180°. An equilateral/isosceles prism instead disperses white light into a spectrum, because n falls with wavelength (Cauchy), so blue bends more than red. Mirage Controls

📖 How Temperature Gradients Bend Light (Mirages) Air's refractive index rises with density. On a hot road, the ground warms the air just above it, so air is hottest and least dense near the surface and cooler/denser higher up. This makes the atmosphere a graded-index medium: n increases smoothly with height. Snell's law applied layer-by-layer gives a conserved quantity n(y)·cosα = const (α = ray angle from horizontal). Tracing this, a downward sky ray bends away from the normal at every layer until it curves back up — a continuous version of Total Internal Reflection. Your brain assumes light travels straight, so it places the source below the road: the shimmering "water" of an inferior mirage. A superior mirage (cold sea below, warm air aloft — an inversion) inverts the gradient and lifts distant objects into view.
Inferior: hot surface, n rises with height → image appears below the ground (the classic "puddle"). Superior: cold surface + warm air aloft (inversion), n falls with height → image floats above the real object.

Rainbow (TIR in Raindrop) Controls

📖 How a Rainbow Forms in a Raindrop A rainbow is a chain of three optical events in one spherical drop: (1) Entry refraction — sunlight entering the drop bends toward the normal (air→water); because of dispersion, violet bends more than red. (2) Total Internal Reflection at the back wall — the internal angle exceeds the critical angle θc = sin⁻¹(1/n) ≈ 48.6° for water, so the light reflects completely back toward the front. (3) Exit refraction — on leaving (water→air) it bends again, separating colours further. Each colour is brightest at its own minimum-deviation "rainbow angle" (≈42° primary, ≈51° secondary). The secondary bow adds a second TIR, reversing the colour order and appearing fainter above the primary. Drag the incidence angle below to steer the ray through the drop.
Primary = one internal TIR (~42°, red on the outside). Secondary = two TIRs (~51°, colours reversed, fainter).

Lens Formula & Magnification

📖 Thin-Lens Equation & Three Principal Rays A thin lens obeys 1/o + 1/i = 1/f (o = object distance, i = image distance, f = focal length). Magnification is M = −i/o — the minus sign means the image is inverted. Three principal rays locate the image: (1) through the lens centre — goes straight; (2) parallel to the axis — exits through the far focal point; (3) through the near focal point — exits parallel. Converging (convex) lenses have f > 0; diverging (concave) lenses have f < 0. Drag the object in the canvas, or use the sliders.
Convex (thicker in the middle, f>0): converges parallel rays — can form real OR virtual images. Concave (thinner in the middle, f<0): diverges rays — always forms a virtual, upright, reduced image.
